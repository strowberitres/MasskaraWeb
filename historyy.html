Origin

The name "Bacolod" is derived from the Hiligaynon word "Buklod," which means "stone hill," and refers to the stone hill where the earliest Austronesians landed in the region. Following an invasion by Moro (Muslim) pirates, the original town was moved to the shore. The historical town is now known as Daan-Banwa, and it is located in Barangay Granada.

On Negros Island's northwesterly shore is where you'll find Bacolod. Its northern, eastern, and southern borders are formed by the cities of Talisay, Murcia, and Bago, respectively. It is a coastal city that the Guimaras Strait borders on the west.
- Location

In 1890, Bacolod was designated as the provincial capital of Negros Occidental. It was the site of a fight between the Negrenses and the Spaniards in 1898, which resulted in the foundation of the Negros Island Republic. This Republic, however, survived only a year.
- Spanish Era

American soldiers invaded Bacolod in March 1899. Educational institutions such as the Rizal Institute and La Consolacion College were founded during this time period. Bacolod was incorporated as a city in 1938.
- American Era

In May 1942, Japanese soldiers invaded Bacolod. In May 1945, American soldiers liberated it. Bacolod has grown from its humble beginnings to become an urbanized metropolis, and it is now regarded as one of the two cleanest and greenest highly urbanized cities in the Philippines.
- World War II

Bacolod is currently famous for its MassKara Festival, a massive annual Mardi Gras-style festival held every third week of October. Each of the city's barangays (districts) competes for the best-costumed group, best in choreography, and other awards. The usage of bright masks with big smiles symbolizes the city's moniker, "City of Smiles."
- Modern Era

The annual MassKara Festival, celebrated on the fourth Sunday of October, is well-known in Bacolod. The event receives its name from the masks used by the dancers. Another significant event is the Panaad sa Negros Festival, which takes place in April. It is a type of thankfulness and a remembrance of a commitment made in return for a good life.
